Policy goal

A small institution doesn’t need a 40-page legal document. It needs clear rules for: (1) who can access what, (2) how reuse is communicated, and (3) when publishing is not appropriate.

1) Access levels (recommended)

  • Public: okay to publish online.
  • Internal: staff/students only (training, in-progress, unreviewed).
  • Restricted: sensitive, culturally controlled, or legally constrained.
CARE reminder (RDA):
“authority to control such data be empowered.”

Restricted can be the correct choice even if “open” is technically possible.

2) Rights statements (make reuse readable)

Use standardized labels where possible so your repository is consistent and machine-readable.

RightsStatements.org:
“provides 12 standardized rights statements…”

This is widely used by cultural heritage aggregators (Europeana, DPLA).

Practical steps

  1. Decide the rights status (copyrighted? public domain? unknown?).
  2. Assign a rights statement (RightsStatements.org) or a license (Creative Commons).
  3. Record it in the inventory row and the project README.

3) Licensing (Creative Commons and CC0)

Creative Commons:
“a standardized way to grant the public permission…”

Pick a license that matches what you actually want people to do.

Common choices

  • CC BY: reuse allowed with attribution.
  • CC BY-NC: reuse allowed noncommercial only.
  • CC0: waive rights as much as possible (public domain dedication).
Smithsonian Open Access (3D):
“Look for the… CC0 icon…”

Even with CC0, good practice is to keep a minimal credit line in metadata.

4) Publishing and IP caution (plain-language)

Don’t assume scanning removes rights. If an object is protected (copyright/design/patent), distributing a high-fidelity 3D model can create legal risk. If rights are unclear, default to internal/restricted until reviewed.

WIPO (3D printing & IP law):
“Copyright will protect the originality of a work…”

When in doubt: document rights uncertainty and control distribution.
